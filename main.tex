\begin{abstract}
For \(X=K3\times E\) the theorem-level physical input used here is the
protected index.  Right-moving localization in the K3 Ramond sector gives
\[
Z_{\mathrm{K3}}=2\phi_{0,1},\qquad
\phi_{0,1}=\sum f(n,l)q^nr^l.
\]
Replacing the protected K3 index by its Borcherds half produces only a
virtual one-particle determinant.  It does not produce a microscopic
Hilbert space.  For virtual super vector spaces
\(\mathbb U_{n,l}\) with \(\sdim\mathbb U_{n,l}=f(n,l)\), set
\(\mathcal V_{(n,l,m)}=\mathbb U_{nm,l}\).  In the Borcherds cusp
chamber the determinant is
\[
\mathcal D_X(Z)
=64q^{1/2}r^{1/2}s^{1/2}
\prod_{(n,l,m)\in\Gamma_{\mathrm{eff}}}
(1-q^nr^ls^m)^{f(nm,l)}
=\Delta_5(Z).
\]
The constant \(64\) is the theta-constant normalization
\([q^{1/2}r^{1/2}s^{1/2}]\Delta_5=64\); hence the monic Borcherds
product is \(D_5=64^{-1}\Delta_5\).

The same coefficients are the signed root supermultiplicities in the
Gritsenko--Nikulin Borcherds--Kac--Moody denominator algebra.  Under
\[
\alpha(n,l,m)=2nf_2-lf_3+2mf_{-2}
\]
one has
\[
\operatorname{den}(\mathfrak g_{\Delta_5})
=64^{-1}\Delta_5(2Z),\qquad
\smult\mathfrak g_{\Delta_5,\alpha(n,l,m)}=f(nm,l)
\]
on the active support.  Thus the denominator algebra supplies the
algebraic square root target; it does not supply compact Hall
correspondences, an orientation, or local collision maps.

The scalar Behrend-weighted branch is imported from
Oberdieck--Pixton, Oberdieck--Pandharipande, and Oberdieck.  In OP's
normalization \(\chi_{10}^{\mathrm{OP}}=D_5^2\), so
\[
\chi_{10}^{\mathrm{OP}}=(64^{-1}\Delta_5)^2
=4096^{-1}\Delta_5^2,\qquad
Z^X_{\mathrm{DT}}=Z^X_{\mathrm{OP}}
=-\left(\chi_{10}^{\mathrm{OP}}\right)^{-1}
=-4096\,\Delta_5^{-2}.
\]
The factor \(4096=64^2\) is a normalization conversion; the minus sign is
the OP scalar convention.  Neither fixes the automorphic reflection
character or the compact orientation monodromy.

A compact realization would have to begin with a holomorphic
\(E_3\)-factorization algebra on \(K3\times E\), specialize it along the
K3 direction to a chiral object on \(\operatorname{Ran}(E)\), solve the
positive-elliptic-degree locality obstruction, and identify the protected
primitive bracket with \(\mathfrak g_{\Delta_5}\).  This paper proves the
virtual determinant, records the imported scalar and denominator
theorems, and formulates that compact Hall/factorization realization as
an open recognition problem.  The final part records the eight
Clery--Gritsenko diagonal-divisor rows and the certificate required
before any boundary row becomes a physical host.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction and claim strength}
\label{sec:introduction-claim-strength}

The local object comes first.  A compact BPS theory on
\(X=K3\times E\), if it exists in the form needed here, should produce
protected local observables before any trace is taken.  After twisting
and passing to the protected index, the positive Hilbert space has
collapsed to a charge-graded virtual object, but locality has not
disappeared: disjoint insertions must still factorize and collisions
must remember extensions.

Thus the natural order is
\[
\begin{gathered}
\hbox{protected locality}
\longrightarrow
\hbox{compact/hybrid factorization}
\longrightarrow
\hbox{primitive recognition}
\\
\longrightarrow
\hbox{Borcherds denominator}
\longrightarrow
\hbox{OP scalar square}
\longrightarrow
\hbox{eight-row host certificates}.
\end{gathered}
\]
The paper follows this order at the level of claims.  Some arrows are
proved in this manuscript, some are imported theorems, and some are
open realization requirements.

\paragraph{Protected locality.}
The protected index is theorem-level.  Right-moving localization of the
unitary K3 sigma model gives
\[
Z_{\mathrm{K3}}=2\phi_{0,1},\qquad
\phi_{0,1}=\sum f(n,l)q^nr^l.
\]
The half-index used by the Borcherds lift is represented only in
\(K_0(\mathrm{sVect}_{\mathrm{fd}})\).  Its coefficients are signed
superdimensions, not dimensions of a constructed compact D-brane Hilbert
space.

\paragraph{Compact and hybrid factorization.}
A compact realization would first supply a charge-completed holomorphic
\(E_3\)-factorization algebra
\[
\mathcal A^{E_3}_X,\qquad X=K3\times E,
\]
then a chain-level K3-to-\(E\) specialization
\[
\mathcal F_X\simeq
\mathrm{Sp}^{\mathrm{ch}}_{K3,E}(\mathcal A^{E_3}_X)
\in\mathrm{Fact}(\operatorname{Ran}(E)).
\]
This is not reconstructed from the scalar partition function or from the
Borcherds product.  There is also a geometric obstruction: a
one-dimensional subscheme with positive elliptic degree projects onto
all of \(E\).  Hence a naive support-local object on
\(\operatorname{Ran}(E)\) sees only projection-finite sectors.  The full
Igusa \(s\)-direction requires either projection-finite locality plus a
separate global Hecke/Fock mode, or a hybrid local/global factorization
theory for wrapped \(E\)-sectors.  This is open.

\paragraph{Primitive recognition.}
The microscopic algebra sought from such a compact realization is the
protected primitive bracket
\[
[\ ,\ ]:\Prim_{\gamma}^{\mathrm{prot}}(\mathcal F_X)
\otimes
\Prim_{\eta}^{\mathrm{prot}}(\mathcal F_X)
\longrightarrow
\Prim_{\gamma+\eta}^{\mathrm{prot}}(\mathcal F_X).
\]
The recognition theorem proved later is conditional: if the compact
Igusa datum \(\mathfrak K_X\) exists, including the oriented Hall object,
protected integration, chiral bar-cobar comparison, and presentation-level
primitive Lie comparison, then
\[
H^0\Prim_{\mathrm{prot}}(\mathcal F_X)
\cong
\mathfrak g_{\Delta_5}
\]
and the chiral cobar object is the formal current envelope
\(\mathsf{Den}_{\Delta_5,E}\).  This is a recognition criterion, not an
existence theorem.

\paragraph{Borcherds denominator.}
The target algebra is imported from the Borcherds--Gritsenko--Nikulin
construction.  With
\[
\alpha(n,l,m)=2nf_2-lf_3+2mf_{-2},
\]
the visible active coefficients satisfy
\[
\smult\mathfrak g_{\Delta_5,\alpha(n,l,m)}=f(nm,l),\qquad
\operatorname{den}(\mathfrak g_{\Delta_5})=64^{-1}\Delta_5(2Z).
\]
This determines signed root supermultiplicities and the denominator
identity.  It does not determine compact Hall constants.

\paragraph{OP scalar square.}
The reduced scalar branch is imported from
Oberdieck--Pixton, Oberdieck--Pandharipande, and Oberdieck:
\[
Z^X_{\mathrm{DT}}(T,Q,P)=Z^X_{\mathrm{OP}}(P,Q,T)
=-4096\,\Delta_5(Z)^{-2}.
\]
The constants are fixed once.  The theta-constant form satisfies
\[
[q^{1/2}r^{1/2}s^{1/2}]\Delta_5=64,\qquad
D_5=64^{-1}\Delta_5.
\]
OP's monic product is \(\chi_{10}^{\mathrm{OP}}=D_5^2\), hence
\[
\chi_{10}^{\mathrm{OP}}=4096^{-1}\Delta_5^2,\qquad
Z^X_{\mathrm{OP}}=-(\chi_{10}^{\mathrm{OP}})^{-1}
=-4096\Delta_5^{-2}.
\]
The number \(64\) is a theta-leading coefficient.  The number
\(-4096\) is the OP scalar normalization.  Neither proves the compact
orientation character.  The comparison
\[
\epsilon_o=\nu_{\Delta_5}
\]
between geometric orientation monodromy and the automorphic reflection
character remains part of the open compact realization datum.

\paragraph{Eight-row host certificates.}
The diagonal-divisor boundary problem concerns the eight
Clery--Gritsenko forms
\[
\Delta_5,\Delta_2,\Delta_1,\Delta_{1/2},
\nabla_3,\nabla_2,\nabla_{3/2},Q_1 .
\]
Rows \(1\)--\(4\) have Gritsenko--Nikulin denominator theorems.  Rows
\(5\)--\(8\) have Clery--Gritsenko products and Govindarajan CHL/WKB
denominator theorems; \(F_7=\nabla_{3/2}\) is on the required
\(\mu_4\)-multiplier cover.  A physical host for any boundary row
requires a certificate fixing the modular group or cover, character,
divisor, cusp Jacobi inputs, product lattice, Weyl vector, real roots,
reflection character, physical charge lattice, one-particle protected
index, orientation, reduced scalar theorem if one is claimed, and
primitive algebra comparison.  Without that certificate the row is only
an automorphic product plus any separately proved denominator theorem.

\section{The physical question}
\label{sec:physical-question}

A charged BPS state is not, in the first instance, a local field.  It
lies in a superselection sector.  Its charge is measured at infinity,
and its existence depends on a stability chamber.  Thus the ordinary
local operator algebra of an untwisted four-dimensional theory is not
the primary object of this paper.

At the level of the protected index the situation is different.  After
taking the cohomology of the chosen supercharge, long multiplets cancel.
The object which remains is not a positive Hilbert space but a
charge-graded virtual object whose Euler characteristic is stable under
deformation.  The index remembers the cancellation law of a unitary
theory, not its positive inner product.

For \(X=K3\times E\) the expected local object in a compact realization
is a supplied holomorphic \(E_3\)-factorization algebra
$$
\mathcal A^{E_3}_X,\qquad X=K3\times E.
$$
The auxiliary notation \(\Phi^{\mathrm{FA}}_3(X)\), when used, is only a
placeholder for this Stage-1 datum; no such functor is constructed in
this paper.  No external \(\Phi^{\mathrm{FA}}_3\) formalism is used here
as an existence theorem unless it supplies the chain-level local
observables, factorization maps, descent, anomaly/QME control, and
compact charge completion required below.  In a compact realization
\(\mathcal A^{E_3}_X\) is the object whose disjoint holomorphic
insertions factorize.  The elliptic curve \(E\) enters only after a
supplied chain-level \(K3\)-specialization whose output is denoted
$$
\mathcal F_X\in\mathrm{Fact}(\operatorname{Ran}(E)).
$$
This is the first compact-realization obstruction.  The
projection-finite sector, where \(p_E(\operatorname{Supp}A)\) is a finite
subset of \(E\), is naturally local on \(\operatorname{Ran}(E)\).  A
one-dimensional sector of positive elliptic degree is not.  If
\(C\subset K3\times E\) has positive elliptic degree, then
\((p_E)_*[C]\) is a positive multiple of \([E]\); hence \(p_E(C)\) is a
one-dimensional closed subset of the irreducible curve \(E\), and so
\(p_E(C)=E\).  Such a class is wrapped or global over \(E\), not
localized at finitely many points of \(E\).

This is the elliptic-degree locality obstruction.  Naive support-local
factorization on \(\operatorname{Ran}(E)\) sees projection-finite
sectors, but by itself it misses the positive \(s\)-direction of the
Igusa product, the direction which records elliptic degree after the
OP/Borcherds variable conversion.  A compact realization must therefore
choose at least one repair of the following kind: projection-finite local
factorization together with separate global Fock/Hecke modes for the
wrapped elliptic-degree sector, or a hybrid local/global wrapped
factorization theory.  This is a realization obstruction.  It is not a
defect in the Borcherds determinant or in the Gritsenko--Nikulin
denominator identity.

Thus, in a realization, the curve-level rule is not imposed externally.
It is the one-dimensional chiral shadow of the holomorphic
\(E_3\)-observable: well-separated protected insertions on \(E\)
multiply, while collisions remember extension and bound-state data.  In
primitive form this is the expected bracket
$$
[\ ,\ ]:
\Prim_{\gamma}^{\mathrm{prot}}(\mathcal F_X)
\otimes
\Prim_{\eta}^{\mathrm{prot}}(\mathcal F_X)
\longrightarrow
\Prim_{\gamma+\eta}^{\mathrm{prot}}(\mathcal F_X).
$$

The Igusa square-root problem is to construct or recognize this
primitive protected algebra.  The determinant below gives the
decategorified signed one-particle index that such a realization would
have:
$$
\sdim\Prim_{\gamma}^{\mathrm{prot}}(\mathcal F_X)
=f(nm,l),
\qquad \gamma=(n,l,m).
$$
The Borcherds--Kac--Moody denominator algebra gives the algebraic
candidate:
$$
H^0\Prim_{\mathrm{prot}}(\mathcal F_X)
\stackrel{?}{\cong}
\mathfrak g_{\Delta_5}.
$$
The theorem-level content of the paper is the determinant and
denominator comparison.  The displayed isomorphism is the compact
\(E_3\)/Hall/chiral realization problem.

The square root is therefore not an analytic ambiguity in
\(\Delta_5^2\).  It is the normalized automorphic determinant of the
virtual protected one-particle index.  The scalar square forgets the
automorphic reflection character; a compact Hall orientation realizing
that character is part of the open datum.

\section{The one-particle index and its determinant}

For the determinant calculation set
$$
Z=\begin{pmatrix}z_1&z_2\\z_2&z_3\end{pmatrix},\qquad
q=e^{2\pi iz_1},\qquad r=e^{2\pi iz_2},\qquad s=e^{2\pi iz_3}.
$$
The Gram-index lattice is
$$
\Gamma_{\mathrm{ind}}=\Z^3,\qquad
\gamma=(n,l,m),\qquad x^\gamma=q^nr^ls^m.
$$
The standard cusp polarization is the ordered limit
$$0<|s|\ll |q|\ll |r|^{-1}\ll1.$$
It determines the semigroup
$$
\Gamma_{\mathrm{eff}}
=\{(n,l,m)\mid m>0,\ n\geq0,\ l\in\Z\}
\cup\{(n,l,0)\mid n>0,\ l\in\Z\}
\cup\{(0,l,0)\mid l<0\}.
$$
Indeed, \(m>0\) is positive because \(s\) is the smallest parameter; if
\(m=0\), then \(n>0\) is positive because \(q\) is next; if \(m=n=0\),
positivity is \(l<0\) because \(|r|^{-1}\ll1\).  This is a chamber
convention for the completed product, not a microscopic charge
positivity statement.

\begin{definition}[Ramond-sector K3 index and its half]
Let $\mathcal H_{\mathrm{RR}}(S)$ be the Ramond--Ramond Hilbert space of
a unitary $\mathcal N=(4,4)$ sigma model on a K3 surface $S$.  Its
central charges are
$$c=\overline c=6.$$
This Hilbert space is a supplied worldsheet input for the protected K3
index.  It is not a construction of the compact \(K3\times E\)
D-brane Hilbert space.
Choose a right-moving Ramond supercharge $Q_R$ with
$$Q_R^2=0,\qquad
\{Q_R,Q_R^\dagger\}=
2\left(\overline L_0-\frac{\overline c}{24}\right).$$
The K3 elliptic genus is the protected supertrace
$$
Z_{\mathrm{K3}}(\tau,z)=
\operatorname{Tr}_{\mathcal H_{\mathrm{RR}}(S)}
(-1)^{F_L+F_R}q^{L_0-c/24}
\overline q^{\,\overline L_0-\overline c/24}r^{J_0}.
$$
The BPS space in degree $(n,l)$ is
$$
\mathcal H^{\mathrm{BPS}}_{n,l}(S):=
H^\bullet\!\left(
\{\psi\mid (L_0-c/24)\psi=n\psi,\ J_0\psi=l\psi\},Q_R
\right).
$$
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Right-moving localization]
Then
$$
Z_{\mathrm{K3}}(\tau,z)=
\sum_{n\geq0,\;l\in\Z}
\sdim\mathcal H^{\mathrm{BPS}}_{n,l}(S)q^nr^l.
$$
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
If $\overline L_0-\overline c/24>0$, the displayed anticommutator pairs
states with opposite $(-1)^{F_R}$ parity.  Their supertrace cancels.
The unpaired states are the $Q_R$-harmonic representatives, hence the
graded superdimension of $Q_R$-cohomology gives the elliptic genus.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Where unitarity enters]
Unitarity is used here before the index is taken: it gives the adjoint
relation which pairs positive right-moving energy states and makes the
supertrace localize.  After passing to the protected index, the object is
virtual.  A coefficient \(f(n,l)\) is a superdimension, not a positive
dimension.  Negative coefficients record an odd excess in the protected
supertrace, or in the chosen \(K_0\)-representative.  They do not
determine the two parity dimensions without an additional Hilbert-space
or denominator-algebra parity datum.
\end{remark}

\begin{definition}[Half-K3 index object]
Eichler--Zagier normalization \cite{EZ:1}:
$$Z_{\mathrm{K3}}(\tau,z)=2\phi_{0,1}(\tau,z),$$
$$\phi_{0,1}(\tau,z)
=\sum_{n\geq 0,\;l\in\Z}f(n,l)q^nr^l.$$
Thus
$$\sdim\mathcal H^{\mathrm{BPS}}_{n,l}(S)=2f(n,l).$$
The class
$$\mathbb U_{n,l}\in K_0(\mathrm{sVect}_{\mathrm{fd}}),
\qquad \sdim\mathbb U_{n,l}=f(n,l),$$
is the normalized half-index object used by the Borcherds lift.  It is
not asserted to be a canonical half of
$\mathcal H^{\mathrm{BPS}}_{n,l}(S)$ as a Hilbert space.
As a \(K_0\)-model one may take \(\mathbb U_{n,l}\) to be
\(\C_{\overline0}^{f(n,l)}\) when \(f(n,l)\ge0\), and
\(\C_{\overline1}^{-f(n,l)}\) when \(f(n,l)<0\).  No canonical choice of
basis, parity lift, or operator product is implied.
$$\phi_{0,1}=r^{-1}+10+r+O(q),\qquad
\frac{f(0,0)}{2}=5.$$
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Fock trace and superdeterminant]
Let
$$V=\bigoplus_{\lambda\in P}V_\lambda$$
be a $P$-graded super vector space with finite-dimensional homogeneous
pieces and let $a_\lambda=\sdim V_\lambda$.  Put
$$\mathcal F(V)=\operatorname{Sym}(V_{\overline 0})
\otimes \bigwedge(V_{\overline 1}).$$
As a formal character,
$$\sch\mathcal F(V):=
\sum_{\mu\in P}\sdim\mathcal F(V)_\mu\,x^\mu
=\prod_{\lambda\in P}(1-x^\lambda)^{-a_\lambda}.$$
The inverse character is the superdeterminant
$$\sdet_V(1-x)=\prod_{\lambda\in P}(1-x^\lambda)^{a_\lambda}.$$
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
$$V_\lambda=\C_{\overline0}:\qquad
\sch\operatorname{Sym}(V_\lambda)=(1-x^\lambda)^{-1}.$$
$$V_\lambda=\C_{\overline1}:\qquad
\sch\bigwedge(V_\lambda)=1-x^\lambda.$$
Tensor product over a homogeneous basis proves the product.
\end{proof}

When \(P=\Gamma_{\mathrm{eff}}\), all products below are taken in the
cusp-completed semigroup algebra determined by
\(0<|s|\ll |q|\ll |r|^{-1}\ll1\).  Every coefficient receives only
finitely many contributions in this completion.

$$x^{(n,l,m)}=q^nr^ls^m.$$
We now form the virtual Borcherds one-particle object.  The third index
\(m\) is the second-quantized/Borcherds degree.  For \(m>0\), the
Hecke--DMVV exponent depends on \(nm\); for \(m=0\), the factors are
cusp-boundary terms \(f(0,l)\).  This is a definition in \(K_0\), not a
microscopic state space.
$$\mathcal V=\bigoplus_{\gamma\in\Gamma_{\mathrm{eff}}}\mathcal V_\gamma,
\qquad
\mathcal V_{(n,l,m)}=\mathcal V_{n,l,m}:=\mathbb U_{nm,l},\qquad
\sdim\mathcal V_{n,l,m}=f(nm,l).$$
The $m>0$ sector is the $K_0$-level virtual half of the DMVV product
\cite{DMVV}, obtained by replacing
$Z_{\mathrm{K3}}=2\phi_{0,1}$ by $\phi_{0,1}$.  The normalized Fock
character in the Borcherds chamber is
$$\sch\mathcal F(\mathcal V)
=\prod_{\gamma\in\Gamma_{\mathrm{eff}}}(1-x^\gamma)^{-\sdim\mathcal V_\gamma}
=\prod_{(n,l,m)\in\Gamma_{\mathrm{eff}}}
(1-q^nr^ls^m)^{-f(nm,l)}.$$
$$\sdet_{\mathcal V}(1-x)
=\prod_{\gamma\in\Gamma_{\mathrm{eff}}}(1-x^\gamma)^{\sdim\mathcal V_\gamma}
=\prod_{(n,l,m)\in\Gamma_{\mathrm{eff}}}
(1-q^nr^ls^m)^{f(nm,l)}.$$
The sector $m>0$ is the normalized half of the second-quantized DMVV
index.  The terms with $m=0$ are cusp and Weyl-chamber boundary factors
of the Borcherds product; the determinant reads only their signed
indices $f(0,l)$.
The normalized cusp-form section below proves the leading coefficient
which fixes the Weyl monomial
$$v_0(Z)=64q^{1/2}r^{1/2}s^{1/2}.$$
$$v_0\notin\bigoplus_{\gamma\in\Gamma_{\mathrm{eff}}}\mathcal V_\gamma.$$

\begin{definition}[Virtual protected determinant]
$$\mathcal{D}_X(Z)
=v_0(Z)\sdet_{\mathcal V}(1-x)
=64q^{1/2}r^{1/2}s^{1/2}
\prod_{(n,l,m)\in\Gamma_{\mathrm{eff}}}
(1-q^nr^ls^m)^{f(nm,l)}.$$
$$\mathcal Z_{\mathrm{BPS}}^{\mathrm{vir}}(Z)
=\mathcal D_X(Z)^{-1}
=\frac{1}{64}q^{-1/2}r^{-1/2}s^{-1/2}
\prod_{(n,l,m)\in\Gamma_{\mathrm{eff}}}
(1-q^nr^ls^m)^{-f(nm,l)}.$$
The word \(BPS\) in this notation is mnemonic for the realization
problem; the object constructed here is the virtual \(K_0\)-determinant.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Determinant normalization]
$$[q^{1/2}r^{1/2}s^{1/2}]\,\mathcal D_X=64,$$
$$\mathcal Z_{\mathrm{BPS}}^{\mathrm{vir}}
=\frac{1}{64}q^{-1/2}r^{-1/2}s^{-1/2}\sch\mathcal F(\mathcal V),$$
$$\mathcal D_X
=64q^{1/2}r^{1/2}s^{1/2}\sdet_{\mathcal V}(1-x).$$
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
$$\sch\mathcal F(\mathcal V)
=\prod_{(n,l,m)\in\Gamma_{\mathrm{eff}}}
(1-q^nr^ls^m)^{-f(nm,l)},\qquad
\sdet_{\mathcal V}(1-x)
=\prod_{(n,l,m)\in\Gamma_{\mathrm{eff}}}
(1-q^nr^ls^m)^{f(nm,l)}.$$
$$v_0(Z)\sdet_{\mathcal V}(1-x)=\mathcal D_X,\qquad
v_0(Z)^{-1}\sch\mathcal F(\mathcal V)
=\mathcal Z_{\mathrm{BPS}}^{\mathrm{vir}}.$$
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Virtuality of the square root]
The displayed construction produces a class
$$
[\mathcal V]\in
K_0(\Gamma_{\mathrm{eff}}\hbox{-graded finite-dimensional super vector spaces})
$$
together with its Fock determinant.  By itself this determinant does not
construct a Hilbert space, a vertex algebra, a holomorphic
\(E_3\)-factorization algebra, or a chiral factorization algebra whose
character is $\mathcal Z_{\mathrm{BPS}}^{\mathrm{vir}}$.

All denominator statements use only the equalities
$$\sdim\mathcal V_{(n,l,m)}=f(nm,l),\qquad
\mathcal D_X=v_0\sdet_{\mathcal V}(1-x).$$
The denominator algebra and formal current envelope are constructed
later from the Borcherds root datum, not from a canonical half of the K3
Hilbert space.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}

\begin{definition}[Projection-finite and wrapped elliptic sectors]
Let \(p_E:K3\times E\to E\) be projection.  A compact Hall or chiral
sector is projection-finite over \(E\) if every object \(A\) in it
satisfies
\[
p_E(\operatorname{Supp}A)\subset F
\]
for some finite subset \(F\subset E\).  A one-dimensional sector is
wrapped over \(E\) if its curve class has positive elliptic degree, i.e.
if the pushforward of its one-cycle to \(E\) is \(b[E]\) with \(b>0\).
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[The elliptic-degree locality obstruction]
\label{lem:elliptic-degree-locality-obstruction}
Let \(C\subset K3\times E\) be a one-dimensional subscheme whose curve
class has positive elliptic degree:
\[
(p_E)_*[C]=b[E],\qquad b>0.
\]
Then \(p_E(C)=E\).  In particular, \(C\) is not localized at finitely
many points of \(E\), and it is not contained in \(p_E^{-1}(U)\) for any
proper open \(U\subsetneq E\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
If \(p_E(C)\) were zero-dimensional, then the pushforward of the
one-cycle \([C]\) to the curve \(E\) would vanish.  This contradicts
\((p_E)_*[C]=b[E]\) with \(b>0\).  Hence \(p_E(C)\) is a
one-dimensional closed subset of the irreducible curve \(E\), and
therefore \(p_E(C)=E\).
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Naive Ran locality misses the elliptic-degree direction]
\label{cor:ranE-locality-misses-elliptic-degree}
A support-local factorization algebra on \(\operatorname{Ran}(E)\),
defined only by separating supports over disjoint opens of \(E\), sees
the projection-finite sector.  It does not by itself realize the full
\(s\)-direction of the Igusa product.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
The variable \(s\) is the Borcherds variable corresponding to the
OP elliptic-degree direction in Proposition~\ref{prop:op-normalization}.
By Lemma~\ref{lem:elliptic-degree-locality-obstruction}, positive
elliptic degree projects onto all of \(E\).  Such objects cannot be
separated by choosing disjoint opens in \(E\).  Therefore ordinary
support-local factorization on \(\operatorname{Ran}(E)\) has no
locality mechanism for the wrapped elliptic-degree sector.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Conditional finite Hall quotient]
\label{prop:sectorial-hall-truncation}
A nonzero additive heart cannot have finite nonzero charge support in
\(\Gamma_{\mathrm{ind}}\).  The active support
\(\Gamma_{\mathrm{act}}\) is not additive.  The finite Hall object is
therefore not a finite active-support heart.

This constructs no Hall object for \(K3\times E\); it records the
finite quotient once the oriented critical Hall object and its
correspondences have been supplied.  Assume the support property, the
HN property, finite type of the
semistable stacks in bounded \(h_S\)-height, and the oriented critical
Hall correspondences.  Then \(F_{\sigma,S}(R)\) and
\(\Gamma_{\sigma,S}^{HN}(R)\) are finite,
\(\mathcal H_{\sigma,S}^{>R}\) is a two-sided Hall ideal, and
\(\mathsf F_R\mathcal H_{\sigma,S}^{or}\) is a finite Hall quotient.
The compact sectorial Hall object is the completed inverse system
$$
\widehat{\mathcal H}_{\sigma,S}^{or}
=\varprojlim_R\mathsf F_R\mathcal H_{\sigma,S}^{or}.
$$
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
If a heart contains an object of nonzero charge \(\gamma\), it contains
all finite direct sums of that object, hence all charges \(k\gamma\).
Since \(\Gamma_{\mathrm{ind}}\) is torsion-free, the charge support is
infinite.  Also \((0,-1,0)\in\Gamma_{\mathrm{act}}\), while
\((0,-2,0)\notin\Gamma_{\mathrm{act}}\), so active support is not
additive.

For semistable classes in \(S\), the strict-sector condition gives
\(h_S(\gamma)\ge c|Z_\sigma(\gamma)|\) for some \(c>0\).  The support
property and discreteness of the numerical lattice make
\(F_{\sigma,S}(R)\) finite.  The function \(h_S\) is positive and
additive on extensions in the sector; hence HN sums of total height
\(\le R\) form a finite set and the subspace of height \(>R\) is a
two-sided Hall ideal.  The inverse limit is the \(h_S\)-adic Hall
completion.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Primitive recognition theorem]
\label{prop:compact-igusa-realization-criterion}
\label{thm:primitive-recognition}
Let \(\mathscr B_{\Delta_5}\) be the Borcherds--Cartan superdatum used by
Gritsenko--Nikulin for the automorphic correction: Cartan
\(\La^{2,1}_{II}\otimes\C\), real simple roots
\(\delta_1,\delta_2,\delta_3\), imaginary simple-root fibres with the
Gritsenko--Nikulin parity split, and bilinear form
\((\alpha_i,\alpha_j)\).  Let \(G(\mathscr B_{\Delta_5})\) denote the
corresponding Borcherds--Kac Lie superalgebra modulo the standard radical
of the completed invariant pairing; this is
\(\mathcal A_{\mathrm{den}}=\mathfrak g_{\Delta_5}\).

Suppose a compact Hall/factorization construction supplies
$$
P_X=H^0\Prim_{\mathrm{prot}}(\mathcal F_X)
$$
after its completed Hopf-pairing radical quotient, with the following
data.
\begin{enumerate}
\item A \(\Gamma_{\mathrm{ind}}\)-graded Lie superalgebra structure on
\(P_X\), finite dimensional in each homogeneous parity piece, and a
Cartan identification
$$P_{X,0}\simeq\La^{2,1}_{II}\otimes\C.$$
\item The degree map
\[
\alpha(n,l,m)=2nf_2-lf_3+2mf_{-2}
\]
identifies the charge grading with the Gritsenko--Nikulin root grading.
\item For every simple-root index \(i\) of
\(\mathscr B_{\Delta_5}\), primitive representatives
\[
e_i^X\in P_{X,\alpha_i},\qquad
f_i^X\in P_{X,-\alpha_i},\qquad
h_i^X\in P_{X,0}
\]
are supplied, preserving the real/imaginary distinction, multiplicity
fibres, and parity map.
\item In the protected Hall/factorization bracket these representatives
satisfy the Borcherds--Kac defining relations for
\(\mathscr B_{\Delta_5}\): Cartan and Chevalley relations, real Serre
relations for the even real simple roots, the imaginary-root
orthogonality relations \((\alpha_i,\alpha_j)=0\Rightarrow
[e_i^X,e_j^X]=[f_i^X,f_j^X]=0\), and the super sign rule.
\item The completed Hopf pairing on the positive and negative primitive
halves has the same radical quotient as the Gritsenko--Nikulin/Kac
construction of \(G(\mathscr B_{\Delta_5})\).
\item \(P_X\) is generated by \(P_{X,0}\) and the simple primitive
representatives \(e_i^X,f_i^X\).
\item The full root-space parity dimensions in \(P_X\) agree with those
of \(G(\mathscr B_{\Delta_5})\).  Consequently, on active positive
Gram degrees,
$$
\sdim P_{X,(n,l,m)}=f(nm,l),
$$
but this signed equality is only a necessary numerical consequence of the
recognition data.
\end{enumerate}
Then
$$
H^0\Prim_{\mathrm{prot}}(\mathcal F_X)
\cong
\mathcal A_{\mathrm{den}}
=\mathfrak g_{\Delta_5}
$$
as charge-graded Lie superalgebras, and its Weyl--Kac--Borcherds
denominator is \(64^{-1}\Delta_5(2Z)\).  If in addition
\(\Theta_{\mathrm{Kos}}\) is supplied, then
$$
\Omega_E^{\mathrm{ch}}C_X\simeq\mathsf{Den}_{\Delta_5,E}.
$$
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The simple primitive representatives and the displayed relations give a
homomorphism
$$
G(\mathscr B_{\Delta_5})\longrightarrow P_X
$$
from the universal Borcherds--Kac presentation.  Generation gives
surjectivity.  The equality of the completed radical quotient and the
full parity dimensions rule out an additional kernel or additional
zero-superdimension root spaces.  Hence the homomorphism is an
isomorphism of charge-graded Lie superalgebras.  The denominator statement
is Theorem~\ref{thm:denominator-algebra-identity}.  The chiral statement
is exactly the quasi-isomorphism \(\Theta_{\mathrm{Kos}}\).
\end{proof}

\end{remark}

\begin{proposition}[The determinant does not determine primitive recognition data]
\label{prop:determinant-does-not-determine-hall-constants}
The scalar square, the Borcherds product, and active support determine
the visible signed product exponents.  After the
Gritsenko--Nikulin denominator theorem, these exponents are the signed
root supermultiplicities.  They do not determine the compact Hall
structure constants, the full parity dimensions, the simple primitive
representatives, the Borcherds--Kac relation verification, or the
Hopf-pairing radical quotient.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The product determines
$$
\smult(\alpha)
=\dim\mathfrak g_{\alpha,\overline0}
-\dim\mathfrak g_{\alpha,\overline1}.
$$
A graded super vector space with the same signed dimensions gives the
same product.  The zero bracket on that graded space gives the same
product but not the Borcherds bracket, since
\([e_i,e_j]\ne0\) in \(\mathcal A_{\mathrm{den}}\) for \(i\ne j\).
A Hall bracket requires compact extension correspondences,
orientation data, protected integration, and a basis of protected
primitives.  The recognition theorem also requires the full parity split,
simple-root representatives, defining relations, generation, and the
radical quotient.  None of these is encoded in the determinant.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[What the denominator determines]
The identity
$$

\left(1-e^{-2\pi i(\alpha,z)}\right)^{\smult(\alpha)}

\rootmult_{\overline1}(\alpha),
$$
and it does not determine the structure constants
$$
[\mathfrak g_\alpha,\mathfrak g_\beta]\to
\mathfrak g_{\alpha+\beta}.
$$
Thus signed root multiplicity is necessary input for primitive
recognition, but it is not a recognition theorem.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Write \(X^\alpha=e^{-2\pi i(\alpha,z)}\).  In the completed positive
root monoid algebra,
$$
\log\prod_{\alpha\in\mathcal R_+}(1-X^\alpha)^{\smult(\alpha)}
=-\sum_{\alpha\in\mathcal R_+}\sum_{k\ge1}
\frac{\smult(\alpha)}{k}X^{k\alpha}.

equivalently by Mobius inversion on each primitive ray.  The recovered
exponent is only
$$
\smult(\alpha)=
\rootmult_{\overline0}(\alpha)-\rootmult_{\overline1}(\alpha).
$$
The difference $a-b$ does not determine the pair $(a,b)$, and no
bilinear bracket appears in the product.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[What the determinant forgets]
\label{thm:protected-denominator-shadow}
Let
$$
\mathcal B=\bigoplus_{\gamma\in\Gamma_{\mathrm{eff}}}\mathcal B_\gamma
$$
be any charge-graded super vector space with finite-dimensional
homogeneous pieces and
$$\sdim\mathcal B_{(n,l,m)}=f(nm,l).$$
Then its Gram-graded Euler/Fock determinant is
$$
64q^{1/2}r^{1/2}s^{1/2}
\prod_{\gamma\in\Gamma_{\mathrm{eff}}}(1-x^\gamma)^{\sdim\mathcal B_\gamma}
=\Delta_5(Z).
$$
If $\mathcal B$ carries a bracket compatible with charge addition, a
parity split, simple-root representatives, or a Hopf pairing, the
determinant forgets those data and remembers the class of
$\mathcal B$ in
$$K_0(\Gamma_{\mathrm{eff}}\hbox{-graded finite super vector spaces}).$$
Thus the determinant of
$\mathcal A_{\mathrm{den}}=\mathfrak g_{\Delta_5}$ is
the Grothendieck shadow of its denominator bracket.  The Borcherds
denominator bracket exists inside \(\mathcal A_{\mathrm{den}}\) by the
Gritsenko--Nikulin construction, but the determinant sees only its
charge-graded Grothendieck class.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The Fock determinant lemma gives
$$
\sdet_{\mathcal B}(1-x)
=\prod_{\gamma\in\Gamma_{\mathrm{eff}}}(1-x^\gamma)^{\sdim\mathcal B_\gamma}.
$$
The hypothesis $\sdim\mathcal B_{(n,l,m)}=f(nm,l)$ identifies this
product with the Borcherds product for $64^{-1}\Delta_5$.  Multiplying
by the vacuum factor gives $\Delta_5$.  A bracket
$$[\mathcal B_\gamma,\mathcal B_{\gamma'}]\to
\mathcal B_{\gamma+\gamma'}$$
does not occur in the determinant formula.  Neither do simple primitive
representatives, parity dimensions, relation constants, or a Hopf-pairing
radical.  The determinant is therefore invariant under replacing
$\mathcal B$ by any other graded super vector space with the same
Grothendieck class.
\end{proof}

\section{Coefficient dictionary and main synthesis}
\label{sec:coefficient-dictionary-main-synthesis}

The coefficient convention is fixed once and for all by
Eichler--Zagier normalization:
$$
Z_{\mathrm{K3}}=2\phi_{0,1},\qquad
\phi_{0,1}(\tau,z)=\sum_{n\ge0,\ l\in\Z}f(n,l)q^nr^l.
$$
Equivalently, if \(\phi_{12,1}\) is the first Fourier--Jacobi
coefficient of Igusa's weight-\(12\) form \(F_{12}\), and if
$$
\delta_{12}(\tau)=q\prod_{n\ge1}(1-q^n)^{24},
$$
then
$$
\phi_{0,1}=\frac{\phi_{12,1}}{\delta_{12}}.
$$
The first terms are
$$
\phi_{0,1}
=(r^{-1}+10+r)
+q(10r^{-2}-64r^{-1}+108-64r+10r^2)
$$
$$
\qquad
+q^2(r^{-3}+108r^{-2}-513r^{-1}+808
-513r+108r^2+r^3)+O(q^3).
$$
Thus
$$
f(0,-1)=1,\qquad f(0,0)=10,\qquad
f(1,1)=-64,\qquad f(1,0)=108.
$$
The weak Jacobi support condition is
$$
f(n,l)=0\quad\hbox{unless}\quad n\ge0,\quad 4n-l^2\ge-1,
$$
and \(f(n,l)=f(n,-l)\).  These integers have three distinct readings in
the paper:
$$
\begin{array}{c|c}
\hbox{object} & \hbox{meaning of }f(nm,l) \\ \hline
\mathcal V_{(n,l,m)} & \hbox{virtual one-particle superdimension} \\
\mathcal D_X & \hbox{Borcherds product exponent} \\
\mathfrak g_{\Delta_5,\alpha(n,l,m)} &
\hbox{signed root supermultiplicity on active support}
\end{array}
$$
The first reading is a protected Euler characteristic.  The second and
third readings are automorphic and denominator-algebra incarnations of
the same integer.  This is not an equality of Hilbert spaces, brackets,
or Hall structure constants.

The synthesis is the following spine:
\[
\begin{gathered}
\hbox{virtual determinant}
\longrightarrow
\hbox{denominator algebra}
\longrightarrow
\hbox{reduced scalar square}
\\
\longrightarrow
\hbox{compact realization problem}
\longrightarrow
\hbox{eight-row certificate extension}.
\end{gathered}
\]
The arrows record the order of comparison.  They are not automatic
implications from a product to a physical host.

\begin{theorem}[Coefficient dictionary and synthesis theorem]
\label{thm:protected-igusa-determinant}
Let \(X=K3\times E\).  Let
\[
\mathbb U_{n,l}\in K_0(\mathrm{sVect}_{\mathrm{fd}}),\qquad
\sdim\mathbb U_{n,l}=f(n,l),
\]
and define the virtual one-particle object in the cusp chamber by
\[
\mathcal V_{(n,l,m)}=\mathbb U_{nm,l},\qquad
(n,l,m)\in\Gamma_{\mathrm{eff}}.
\]
Then the theorem-level and conditional content of the paper is exactly
as follows.

First, the virtual determinant is the theta-normalized Igusa square root:
\[
\mathcal D_X(Z)
=64q^{1/2}r^{1/2}s^{1/2}
\prod_{(n,l,m)\in\Gamma_{\mathrm{eff}}}
(1-q^nr^ls^m)^{f(nm,l)}
=\Delta_5(Z).
\]
The numerical factor \(64\) is the theta-constant leading coefficient
\([q^{1/2}r^{1/2}s^{1/2}]\Delta_5\); the monic Borcherds product is
\[
D_5=64^{-1}\Delta_5.
\]

Second, under the Lorentzian degree map
\[
\alpha(n,l,m)=2nf_2-lf_3+2mf_{-2},
\qquad
q^nr^ls^m=\exp(-\pi i(\alpha(n,l,m),z)),
\]
the same active coefficients are the visible signed root
supermultiplicities of the Gritsenko--Nikulin denominator algebra:
\[
\smult\mathfrak g_{\Delta_5,\alpha(n,l,m)}
=f(nm,l)
\qquad ((n,l,m)\in\Gamma_{\mathrm{act}}),
\]
and
\[
\operatorname{den}(\mathfrak g_{\Delta_5})
=64^{-1}\Delta_5(2Z).
\]
This is the Gritsenko--Nikulin denominator theorem.  It determines the
signed root supermultiplicities in the fixed Borcherds datum; it does
not determine compact Hall constants or microscopic brackets.

Third, for the reduced Hilbert-scheme DT/PT/OP scalar branch,
\[
Z^X_{\mathrm{DT}}(T,Q,P)=Z^X_{\mathrm{OP}}(P,Q,T)
=-4096\,\Delta_5(Z)^{-2}.
\]
Here
\[
\chi_{10}^{\mathrm{OP}}=D_5^2=(64^{-1}\Delta_5)^2
=4096^{-1}\Delta_5^2,
\qquad
Z^X_{\mathrm{OP}}=-(\chi_{10}^{\mathrm{OP}})^{-1};
\]
hence \(-4096=-(64)^2\) is the OP scalar sign together with the
theta-to-monic normalization.  It is not an orientation character.
Thus, with
\[
\mathcal Z_{\mathrm{BPS}}^{\mathrm{vir}}=\mathcal D_X^{-1},
\]
one has
\[
Z^X_{\mathrm{DT}}=Z^X_{\mathrm{OP}}
=-4096\,(\mathcal Z_{\mathrm{BPS}}^{\mathrm{vir}})^2.
\]

Fourth, the stronger microscopic comparison
\[
H^0\Prim_{\mathrm{prot}}(\mathcal F_X)
\cong
\mathfrak g_{\Delta_5}
\]
is open unless the compact datum of
Definition~\ref{def:compact-igusa-realization-datum} is constructed.
If such a datum is constructed, Theorem~\ref{thm:primitive-recognition}
identifies its primitive denominator with \(64^{-1}\Delta_5(2Z)\).

Fifth, any claimed compact realization must also satisfy three
consequences.
\begin{enumerate}
\item A naive support-local factorization object on
\(\operatorname{Ran}(E)\) sees only projection-finite sectors.  If a
one-dimensional subscheme of \(K3\times E\) has positive elliptic
degree, its projection to \(E\) is all of \(E\).  Thus the full
\(s\)-direction of the Igusa product requires either a separate
global Fock/Hecke mode or a hybrid local/global wrapped factorization
theory.
\item The automorphic character \(\nu_{\Delta_5}\) is theorem-level
data of \(\Delta_5\), and \(\Delta_5^2\) forgets it.  A compact Hall
orientation must construct a strong orientation character
\(\epsilon_o\) and prove
\[
\epsilon_o|_{W^{(2)}(\Lambda^{2,1}_{II})}
=\nu_{\Delta_5}|_{W^{(2)}(\Lambda^{2,1}_{II})}.
\]
The constants \(64\), \(4096\), and the OP minus sign do not solve this
orientation-monodromy problem.
\item A primitive comparison with \(\mathfrak g_{\Delta_5}\) is a
presentation-level recognition theorem, not a consequence of signed
dimensions.  One must supply primitive representatives for the real and
imaginary simple roots, their parities, the Chevalley, real Serre and
orthogonality relations, generation, the completed Hopf pairing and the
same radical quotient.  Under these hypotheses the Borcherds--Kac
universal presentation gives the isomorphism with
\(\mathfrak g_{\Delta_5}\); without them the determinant fixes only a
Grothendieck class.
\end{enumerate}

Finally, the diagonal-divisor rows extend this same logic.  A row is
promoted beyond its automorphic product only by a row certificate fixing
the modular group or cover, character, divisor, Jacobi seed, product
lattice, Weyl chamber, reflection character, scalar theorem if one is
claimed, orientation data, one-particle index, and primitive algebra
comparison.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The Fock trace calculation gives
\[

=64q^{1/2}r^{1/2}s^{1/2}\sdet_{\mathcal V}(1-x).
\]
The Borcherds--Gritsenko--Nikulin product formula for the input
\(\phi_{0,1}\) identifies this product with \(\Delta_5\), as recorded in
Proposition~\ref{prop:bgn-identification}.  The leading coefficient
\(64\) is fixed in the theta-normalized Fourier expansion of
\(\Delta_5\), hence \(D_5=64^{-1}\Delta_5\) is the monic product.

The Lorentzian monomial identity and pairing computation are in
Section~\ref{sec:duality-principle} and the Weyl-chamber section.  The
root-supermultiplicity and denominator identity are
Theorem~\ref{thm:denominator-algebra-identity}; the argument \(2Z\)
comes from passing from
\(\exp(-\pi i(\alpha,z))\) to the usual denominator character
\(\exp(-2\pi i(\alpha,z))\).

The reduced scalar identity is Theorem~\ref{thm:op-square}, with the
normalization conversion of Proposition~\ref{prop:op-normalization}.
Since Oberdieck--Pixton use
\(\chi_{10}^{\mathrm{OP}}=D_5^2\) and prove
\(Z^X_{\mathrm{OP}}=-(\chi_{10}^{\mathrm{OP}})^{-1}\), the theta
normalization gives
\[

\begin{theorem}[Andrianov factorisation]
\label{thm:andrianov-factor}

$\Delta_{10}^{\theta}$,
$$L_{\mathrm{spin}}(s,\Delta_{10}^{\theta})

\begin{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Central-zero cancellation]
\label{prop:sk-central-zero}
With
$$\Lambda(g,s):=(2\pi)^{-s}\Gamma(s)L(s,g),$$
one has
$$\Lambda(g,s)=-\Lambda(g,18-s),\qquad L(9,g)=0.$$
Hence the possible pole of $\zeta(s-8)L(s,g)$ at $s=9$ is cancelled.
At $s=10$ the residue is
$$\operatorname*{Res}_{s=10}L_{\mathrm{spin}}(s,\Delta_{10}^{\theta})
=\zeta(2)L(10,g)=\frac{\pi^2}{6}L(10,g).$$
Moreover \(L(10,g)\ne0\).  Thus
\(L_{\mathrm{spin}}(s,\Delta_{10}^{\theta})\) has a simple pole at
\(s=10\) with non-zero residue.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For a level-one cusp form of weight $18$, the root number is
$(-1)^{18/2}=-1$.  Thus the completed $L$-function is odd about
$s=9$, and the central value vanishes.  The factor $\zeta(s-8)$ has
its only possible pole at $s=9$, so the product
$\zeta(s-8)L(s,g)$ is regular there.  At $s=10$ the factor
$\zeta(s-9)$ has residue $1$, while $\zeta(s-8)=\zeta(2)$.
Write
$$L_p(s,g)=(1-\alpha_pp^{-s})^{-1}(1-\beta_pp^{-s})^{-1}.$$
Deligne's Ramanujan--Petersson theorem gives
$$|\alpha_p|=|\beta_p|=p^{17/2}.$$
At \(s=10\),
$$|\alpha_pp^{-10}|=|\beta_pp^{-10}|=p^{-3/2},$$
so the Euler product converges absolutely and has no zero.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Product cone and chamber compatibility]
\label{lem:gn-product-chamber-compatibility}
Let \(t=2,3,4\).  If
$$f_t(nm,l)\ne0,\qquad (n,l,m)\in\Gamma_t^{\mathrm{prod}},$$
then
$$
(\alpha_t(n,l,m),\rho_t)\le0.
$$
For \(t=2,3\) the inequality is strict.  For \(t=4\), equality occurs
only on the parabolic ray
$$\alpha_t(n,l,m)\in\Q_{>0}\rho_4.$$
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The Jacobi support satisfies
$$4tnm-l^2\ge -1.$$
Since
$$
(\alpha_t(n,l,m),\rho_t)=-2n+l-2m,
$$
the chamber inequality is \(l\le2n+2m\).  For \(t=2,3\), Gritsenko--
Nikulin Lemma 2.5 gives the required strict chamber condition on the
active support.  For \(t=4\), the proof of Gritsenko--Nikulin (1.33)
gives the same condition, with equality only in the parabolic case.
Equivalently, equality and \(4tnm-l^2\ge -1\) force
$$n=m,\qquad l=4n,$$
so
$$\alpha_4(n,4n,n)=8n\rho_4.$$
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Gritsenko--Nikulin denominator rows]
\label{thm:gn-denominator-234}
Let $t=2,3,4$ and put
$$
\begin{array}{c|c|c}
t&F(t)&\phi_t\\ \hline
2&\Delta_2&\phi_{0,2}\\
3&\Delta_1&\phi_{0,3}\\
4&\Delta_{1/2}&\phi_{0,4}
\end{array}
$$
Let
$$
\mathfrak g_t^{\mathrm{GN}}
\quad (t=2,3,4)
$$
be the generalized Borcherds--Kac--Moody superalgebra whose Lorentzian
root lattice is $M_t$, whose Weyl group is
$W_t=W^{(2)}(M_t)$, whose Weyl vector is
$$
\rho_t=\left(\frac{1}{2t},\frac12,\frac{1}{2t}\right),
$$
and whose simple real roots are $P_t=P_{t,II,\overline0}$ from
Proposition~\ref{prop:gn-chambers-234}.  Let $\mathcal M_t$ denote
the corresponding chamber.  Then
$$
P_{t,\overline1}=\varnothing,\qquad P_{t,\overline0}=P_t,
$$
so every simple real root is even and
$\epsilon_t(s_\delta)=-1$ for every $\delta\in P_t$; equivalently
$\epsilon_t(w)=\det(w)$ on $W_t$.

Let
$$
\phi_t(\tau,z)=\sum f_t(n,l)q^nr^l .
$$
The integers \(N_t(a)\) are the additive-side coefficients in
Gritsenko--Nikulin (5.1), equivalently the signed supermultiplicities of
imaginary simple roots.  They are not the product exponents \(f_t(nm,l)\).
In the Gritsenko--Nikulin exponential normalization
$e_t^{(n,l,m)}=q^nr^ls^{tm}$, the Weyl--Kac--Borcherds denominator
identity is
$$
\begin{aligned}
\operatorname{den}(\mathfrak g_t^{\mathrm{GN}})
&=\sum_{w\in W_t}\epsilon_t(w)
 \left(e_t^{w(\rho_t)}
 +\sum_{a\in\R_{++}\mathcal M_t\cap M_t}
 N_t(a)e_t^{w(\rho_t+a)}\right)\\
&=e_t^{\rho_t}
\prod_{(n,l,m)\in\Gamma_t^{\mathrm{prod}}}
\left(1-e_t^{(n,l,m)}\right)^{f_t(nm,l)}
=F(t)(Z).
\end{aligned}
$$
Equivalently,
$$
\operatorname{den}(\mathfrak g_2^{\mathrm{GN}})=\Delta_2(Z),\qquad
\operatorname{den}(\mathfrak g_3^{\mathrm{GN}})=\Delta_1(Z),\qquad
\operatorname{den}(\mathfrak g_4^{\mathrm{GN}})=\Delta_{1/2}(Z).
$$
Thus, for rows $j=2,3,4$,
$$
C'_j=1,\qquad a_j=1,\qquad
L_j=M_j,\qquad \alpha_j=\alpha_j^{\mathrm{GN}}
$$
in this normalization.  The imaginary root spaces are determined here
only as signed multiplicity spaces:
$$
\sdim(\mathfrak g_{t,\alpha_t(n,l,m)})
=f_t(nm,l)
$$
on the product side, with imaginary simple-root superdimensions
$N_t(a)$ on the Weyl-sum side.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Gritsenko--Nikulin \cite[Section 5.1]{GNII} state that almost all
identities proved earlier in their paper give denominator formulae for
Lorentzian Kac--Moody algebras.  Their formula (5.1) is exactly the
displayed Weyl--Kac--Borcherds identity on
$M_t=U(4t)\oplus\langle2\rangle$, with
$e^{(n,l,m)}=q^nr^ls^{tm}$.

For the cases $(t,II,\overline0)$, $t=2,3,4$, they give
$$
W_{t,II,\overline0}=W^{(2)}(M_t),\qquad
\rho_{t,II,\overline0}
=\left(\frac{1}{2t},\frac12,\frac{1}{2t}\right),
$$
and
$$
P_{t,II,\overline0}(\mathcal M_{t,II,\overline0})
=P_{t,II,\overline0}(\mathcal M_{t,II,\overline0})_{\overline0}.
$$
Hence the odd simple-real-root set is empty and the Weyl character is
the ordinary reflection sign on the simple real reflections.  The roots
in $P_t$ have square $2$, and the generalized Cartan matrix is
$((\delta,\delta'))_{\delta,\delta'\in P_t}$; for $t=4$ this is the
infinite symmetric parabolic matrix recorded by Gritsenko--Nikulin.
For real roots \(4tn-l^2=-1\), the relevant Jacobi coefficient is \(1\):
this is Gritsenko--Nikulin Lemma 2.5 for \(t=2,3\) and the proof of
Gritsenko--Nikulin (1.33) for \(t=4\).

The product factors are the full-paramodular products
\cite[(2.21), (2.20), (2.11)]{GNII}, already displayed in
Proposition~\ref{prop:gn-paramodular-row-data}.  Their prefactors are
exactly $e_t^{\rho_t}$.  Therefore the denominator functions are
$\Delta_2$, $\Delta_1$, and $\Delta_{1/2}$ with no further scalar or
argument rescaling in the $e_t$ normalization.
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Formal product algebra of a row]
For a row $j$ with product-degree function $\mu_j$, let
$$
\mathcal V_j^{\mathrm{prod}}
=\bigoplus_{\lambda\in L_j^{\mathrm{prod}},\ \lambda>0}
\mathcal V_{j,\lambda}
$$
be any graded super vector space with finite-dimensional homogeneous
pieces and
$$
\sdim\mathcal V_{j,\lambda}=\mu_j(\lambda).

Choose a Grothendieck representative: the even part has dimension $\mu_j(\lambda)$ when
$\mu_j(\lambda)>0$, the odd part of dimension $-\mu_j(\lambda)$ when

$$[\mathcal V_{j,\lambda},\mathcal V_{j,\lambda'}]=0.$$
Its formal current envelope on $E$ is
$$
\mathsf{Prod}_{j,E}
:=U_E^{\mathrm{ch}}
\left(\mathcal V_j^{\mathrm{prod}}\otimes\mathcal O_E\right).
$$
\end{definition}

envelope is abelian; it carries no Weyl-sum side, no BKM bracket, and no
physical protected BPS interpretation.

In all eight cases the formal determinant is the corresponding monic
automorphic product:
$$
e^{\rho_j}\sdet_{\mathcal V_j}(1-e)
=D_j(Z),
$$
where $D_1=64^{-1}\Delta_5$ and $D_j=F_j$ for the displayed
Clery--Gritsenko product normalizations $j>1$,
with the normalizations stated in
Propositions~\ref{prop:gn-paramodular-row-data},
\ref{prop:gc-row-data}, and
\ref{prop:congruence-product-degree-lattices}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For $j=1$, Theorem~\ref{thm:denominator-algebra-identity} gives the
Borcherds denominator algebra and Proposition~\ref{prop:formal-current-envelope} gives
the formal current envelope.  For $j=2,3,4$,
Theorem~\ref{thm:gn-denominator-234} gives the BKM brackets and their
denominator identities.

For $j=5,6,7,8$, Proposition~\ref{prop:congruence-product-degree-lattices}
constructs the degree lattice, the positive product support, the
prefactor, and the signed exponent $\mu_j$.  The super vector space
$\mathcal V_j^{\mathrm{prod}}$ realizes these signed dimensions.  The
Fock determinant lemma gives
$$
e^{\rho_j^{\mathrm{prod}}}
\prod_\lambda(1-e^\lambda)^{\sdim\mathcal V_{j,\lambda}}
=
e^{\rho_j^{\mathrm{prod}}}
\prod_\lambda(1-e^\lambda)^{\mu_j(\lambda)}
=F_j(Z).
$$
The formal current enveloping construction gives a chiral algebra on
\(E\) from this abelian Lie superalgebra.  It is not a compact BPS
factorization algebra.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Nonabelian promotion test for $F_5,F_6,F_7,F_8$]
\label{prop:congruence-denominator-test}
Suppose that one of the congruence product rows
$$j\in\{5,6,7,8\}$$
is the denominator of a generalized Borcherds--Kac--Moody
superalgebra on the product lattice $L_j^{\mathrm{prod}}$.  Then the
following additional data must exist:
$$
\left(P_j,\mathcal M_j,W_j,\epsilon_j,\rho_j,N_j(a)\right).
$$
They must satisfy
$$
W_j=\langle s_\delta\mid\delta\in P_j\rangle,\qquad
\mathcal M_j=\{x\mid(x,\delta)\leq0\ \hbox{for all }\delta\in P_j\},
$$
$$
(\rho_j,\delta)=-\frac{(\delta,\delta)}{2}
\qquad(\delta\in P_j),
$$
and the Weyl--Kac--Borcherds identity
$$
\begin{aligned}
F_j(Z)
&=\sum_{w\in W_j}\epsilon_j(w)
\left(e^{w(\rho_j)}
+\sum_{a\in L_j^{\mathrm{prod}}\cap\R_{>0}\mathcal M_j}
N_j(a)e^{w(\rho_j+a)}\right)\\
&=e^{\rho_j^{\mathrm{prod}}}
\prod_{\lambda\in\alpha_j^{\mathrm{prod}}(\mathcal D_j)}
\left(1-e^\lambda\right)^{\mu_j(\lambda)}.
\end{aligned}
$$
In particular the product already fixes the signed root multiplicity
function $\mu_j$ and the product-prefactor degree
$$
\rho_5^{\mathrm{prod}}=\rho_6^{\mathrm{prod}}=\rho_7^{\mathrm{prod}}
=\alpha_1(\tfrac12,\tfrac12,\tfrac12),\qquad
\rho_8^{\mathrm{prod}}=\alpha_2(\tfrac14,\tfrac12,\tfrac14),
$$
but it does not fix $P_j$, $\mathcal M_j$, $W_j$, $\epsilon_j$, or the
imaginary simple-root coefficients $N_j(a)$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The equalities in Proposition~\ref{prop:congruence-product-degree-lattices}
construct only the monomial degree and the total exponent
$\mu_j(\lambda)$.  A denominator theorem has a second side: the Weyl
alternating sum.  That side requires a reflection chamber, a set of
simple real roots, a reflection character, and the imaginary
simple-root contribution.

The Gritsenko--Nikulin chambers for $M_1$ and $M_2$ do not follow by
notation.  The forms $\nabla_3$, $\nabla_2$, $\nabla_{3/2}$, and
$Q_1$ are congruence products with non-infinity cusp factors, whereas
$\Delta_5$ and $\Delta_2$ are full-paramodular denominator forms.
Clery--Gritsenko construct the automorphic product, character, and
diagonal divisor on
$$U\oplus U(N_j)\oplus\langle-2t_j\rangle,$$
but their product theorem does not identify the simple real roots or
prove a Weyl anti-invariant additive expansion on $M_{t_j}$.  Therefore
the displayed six-tuple is an additional theorem to be proved, not a
consequence of the product formula.
\end{proof}

$$
F(Z)^{1/k}
=a^{1/k}q^{\alpha/k}r^{\beta/k}s^{\gamma/k}
\exp\left(\frac1k\log(1+O(q,r,s))\right),
$$
where \(a^{1/k}\) is the positive real root and the logarithm is the
formal logarithm in the completed product algebra.  This defines a
formal branch.  It does not define a modular form until the relevant
metaplectic or multiplier-cover character has been constructed.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Reconstituted fractional-power comparison test]
\label{prop:fractional-power-comparison-test}
The earlier fractional-power comparison symbols
\[
\Phi^{\mathrm{cmp}}_5,\qquad
\Phi^{\mathrm{cmp}}_7,\qquad
\Phi^{\mathrm{cmp}}_8
\]
do not label Clery--Gritsenko rows.  The only source-compatible
statement retained here is the following conditional test.

The displayed comparison weight of \(\Phi^{\mathrm{cmp}}_5\) is
\(1/2\).  If it is presented as a square root, the parent form must have
weight \(1\), and the root must live on the pullback of the parent group
to the genus-two metaplectic cover with a genuine half-integral
multiplier.  A weight-\(5\) parent would produce a weight-\(5/2\) square
root and does not certify this displayed comparison slot.

If the comparison symbol \(\Phi^{\mathrm{cmp}}_7\) is supplied with its
displayed comparison weight \(1/4\), then it must be a genuine
quarter-integral-weight form on a multiplier cover.  If it is presented
as a fourth root of a parent form with exact order-four character, an
order-eight cover is not forced by character theory.  If a character
root \(\psi\) with \(\psi^4=\chi\) exists after pullback and \(\chi\)
has exact order \(4\), then the character part has order divisible by
\(16\), unless an explicit automorphy-cocycle relation reduces it.  No
such relation is constructed here.
The \(\mu_4\)-cover of the Clery--Gritsenko row
\(F_7=\nabla_{3/2}\), whose weight is \(3/2\), is not a modular-root
certificate for a comparison object \(\Phi^{\mathrm{cmp}}_7\) of weight
\(1/4\).  That comparison object needs its own parent, cover, cocycle,
and character-root computation.

If a comparison form \(\Phi^{\mathrm{cmp}}_8\) has weight \(0\), the
canonical branch of Definition~\ref{def:canonical-fractional-branch}
is only a formal logarithmic branch.  By itself it gives no
finite-order modular multiplier and no chiral square-root theorem.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The first two assertions are necessary metaplectic-cover conditions:
a fractional power of an automorphic product is modular only after the
automorphy factor, character, and divisor multiplicities lift to a
genuine character on a cover; Weil and Shimura give the classical
background for such metaplectic phenomena \cite{Weil1964},
\cite{Shimura1973}.  The stated character-order obstruction is
elementary: if \(\chi\) has exact order \(4\) and \(\psi^4=\chi\), then
\(\psi\) cannot have order \(8\), since \(\psi^4\) would have order at
most \(2\); absent a further cocycle relation, order \(16\) is the first
possible character order.  In the weight-zero case the formal branch
supplies no metaplectic weight contribution; modularity would have to
come entirely from divisor divisibility and a character or automorphy
cocycle root.  A formal root can be chosen in a chamber, but this is not
a modular or chiral construction.
\end{proof}

\begin{openproblem}[Fractional comparison-row physical-host certificate]
\label{op:fractional-comparison-row-host}
For a fractional comparison row, construct a modular-root certificate
\(\mathscr R_j\) and the five-level physical-host certificate of
Definition~\ref{def:physical-host-certificate}.  The root certificate
must fix the parent form, root exponent, exact group or cover,
multiplier character and its order, automorphy cocycle, divisor
multiplicities, branch, product normalization, and source theorem.
The reduced scalar level, if claimed, must produce a compact
Calabi--Yau, orbifold Calabi--Yau, or CHL background with oriented
reduced obstruction theory and prove
$$
Z_j^{\mathrm{red}}=C_j(\Phi^{\mathrm{cmp}}_j)^{-2}
$$
in the fixed chamber and cover.  The compact Hall/chiral level must
construct the protected one-particle index and primitive bracket
comparison for the same row.

Borcea--Voisin and higher-dimensional curve-counting constructions
\cite{BORCEA}, \cite{VOISIN}, \cite{OhThomasI}, \cite{OhThomasII},
\cite{BOPY} are possible sources for such a certificate.  This paper
does not assert that they supply it.  Until \(\mathscr R_j\) and the
relevant certificate entries are constructed, the symbols
\(\Phi^{\mathrm{cmp}}_5,\Phi^{\mathrm{cmp}}_7,\Phi^{\mathrm{cmp}}_8\)
are formal comparison symbols only: no Clery--Gritsenko row,
finite-order multiplier, denominator identity, CY/DT partition theorem,
or chiral power follows.
\end{openproblem}
